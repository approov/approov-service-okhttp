Start Here
1 - Create a Project, not a chat
Every time you open a new Claude chat, it starts with zero memory. It 
doesn't know your name, your work, your goals, or how you like to 
communicate. You spend the first few messages re-explaining yourself, 
or you don't, and Claude gives you something generic that doesn't fit 
how you actually work.
Projects fix this. A Project is a persistent workspace where Claude keeps 
context across every conversation inside it. You set it up once and every 
session that follows starts with Claude already knowing who you are.
Go to Claude, click Projects in the sidebar, and create a new one. Name 
it something like "Work" or "Personal" depending on how you plan to use 
it. Everything that follows goes inside this Project.
2 - Tell Claude who you are
Before Claude can help you well, it needs to understand you. Most 
people skip this step entirely and wonder why Claude gives them 
answers that feel slightly off.
Paste this into your Project and fill in every field honestly. The more 
specific you are, the better every single response becomes.
My name is [your name].
I work as [your role or profession]. My main responsibilities are [2-3 
Right now my biggest goals are [1-3 specific goals you're working towar
I use Claude mostly for [list your main use cases — writing, research, 
My background and knowledge level: [what you know well, what you're lea
How I like to receive information: [e.g. direct and concise / detailed 
Things I don't want: [e.g. don't add disclaimers, don't use corporate l

Save this inside your Project's knowledge base. Claude will read it at the 
start of every conversation in this Project.

Claude Is Not What You Think
4 - Claude is not a search engine
Most people use Claude the way they use Google. They type a question 
and wait for an answer. That is the lowest-value way to use it.
Claude is not a retrieval tool. It is a thinking partner. It doesn't just pull 
information, it reasons, synthesizes, argues, and builds on context. The 
moment you treat it like a search engine, you cut its usefulness by 80 
percent.
Stop asking Claude what something is. Start asking Claude to help you 
think through something.
Instead of: "What is prompt caching?"
Try: "I'm building a workflow that calls Claude 20 times per session. Walk 
me through how prompt caching works and whether it would actually 
reduce my costs given that context."
The second prompt gives Claude a problem to solve with you. The first 
gives it a definition to recite.

This is one of the most powerful techniques almost nobody uses. Before 
Claude starts any complex task, tell it to gather information from you 
first.

voice. The output is grammatically correct and completely wrong in tone. 
It sounds like AI because it is.

your patterns, not just describe your style. After that analysis, it writes 
like you, not like a polished corporate assistant.

8 - Extended Thinking
Most Claude users have never turned this on. Extended Thinking is a 
mode where Claude reasons through a problem step by step before 
giving you an answer, instead of going straight to the output.
For simple tasks, you don't need it. For complex decisions, analysis, or 
any question where you want Claude to actually think rather than 
pattern-match, turn it on.
In Claude, click the brain icon before sending your message. Or add this 
to your prompt:

Work through the problem step by step, show your reasoning, identify wh
The difference in output quality on hard questions is significant.

How to Spend Fewer Tokens and Get More
10 - Specify the output length
Claude's default is to write as much as it thinks is appropriate. That is 
usually more than you need, which means more tokens used, more time 
spent reading, and more noise in the output.
Tell Claude exactly how long you want the answer before it starts.

not actual comprehension.

isn't clear enough yet. This prompt works for anything from investing to

The Actual Point

What it has is infinite patience, broad knowledge, and the ability to think 
through problems from angles you haven't considered.

questions. They are the ones who have set it up to understand them, 
who give it real context, and who know how to use it as a partner rather 
than a dispenser.
Most people will read this and keep opening Claude the same way they
